Introduction¶

In this project we'll be analizing data associated with 100k medical appointments in Brazil. In particular, we are interested in finding trends among the patients who show and how they differ from those who do not show. Different information about the patient is included in each row - ScheduledDay tells us on what day of week the patient set up their appointment, Neighborhood indicates the location of the hospital, Scholarship indicates whether or not the patient is enrolled in Brasilian welfare program Bolsa Famlia. Other data are gender, age, type of chronic disease if any, recieved or not SMS notification etc.

The most important research question is, what features are important for us to take into account in order to predict if a patient will show up for their scheduled appointment or will most likely skip it?

Conclusions¶

As we can see from the results of our investigation, most of the given factors such as gender, alcoholism, handicap or neighbourhood have minimal impact on patient's desicion whether to show or not at their medical appointment. Overal, age seems to be the most important factor - we figured out that people of age 34.317666562121957 are likely to miss their appointment, and patients of age 37.790064393252315 are not likely to miss an apointment they scheduled. This happens mostly on Friday or Saturday (a posssible reason why data from Sunday is missing is that it is probably a day off). People who have financial support from the government also tend to show up less frequently. Sending SMS suprisingly does not help to ensure that a patient will arrive as agreed. Other data to consider interesting for the purposes of our further researh are diabetes, hipertension and handicap.

It is necessary to mention that this research had some obvious limitations such as only 27 available dates of observations, covering three month only, which is not enough to make trustful conclusions about global or local trends. There are also some missing data and no useful features like, for example, history of patients' previous no-shows or the initial purposes of scheduled visits.
